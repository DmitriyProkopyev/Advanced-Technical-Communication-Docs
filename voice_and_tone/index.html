    Voice vs. Tone vs. Personality

    The emotional background of a message communicates information alongside its content. That is why it is important to choose the right voice, tone, and personality.

    Concept Definition How it differs Example in technical writing
    Voice The consistent identity of the writing. Stays mostly stable across documents and situations. A docs site always sounds concise, clear, and helpful.
    Tone The attitude used in a specific piece or moment. Changes depending on context, audience, or purpose. A setup guide sounds encouraging; an outage notice sounds urgent and direct.
    Personality The broader character readers sense from the writing. Describes the traits behind the voice, not just a single message. The docs feel calm, confident, and practical.

    Voice

    First, follow this checklist to define the voice of your product:

    • Identify the product’s voice in one short sentence of characteristics. Example "confident, collaborative, data‑driven".
    • Decide where to intentionally use domain jargon vs. plain language.
    • Design a consistent reader reference policy. Example: "you" as default, limited "we" where partnership is intended.

    Tone

    Then, use the four axis below to variate the tone to be appropriate to the context:

    Dimension Definition How it changes tone Example
    Formality How serious, structured, and professional the language sounds. Higher formality uses precise wording, fewer contractions, and less casual phrasing; lower formality sounds more conversational. Formal: "Please submit the report by Friday." Informal: "Please send it over by Friday."
    Polarity The emotional direction of the tone: positive, neutral, or negative. Positive tone feels encouraging, neutral tone stays factual, and negative tone signals problems or criticism. Positive: "Great progress so far." Neutral: "The build completed successfully." Negative: "The deployment failed."
    Urgency How strongly the writing signals immediacy or time sensitivity. High urgency pushes action now; low urgency gives a calmer, less time-critical feel. Low urgency: "Review this when convenient." High urgency: "Fix this before the next release."
    Empathy How much the writing shows understanding of the reader’s situation or frustration. High empathy acknowledges pain, confusion, or difficulty; low empathy is more detached and purely functional. Low empathy: "Incorrect input." High empathy: "Sorry about the error, let’s fix it together."

    Identify the key scenarios the readers are likely to encounter and define the right tone for them.

    Personality

    Finally, define personality for your documentation. Do it by aligning it with one or several common archetypes such as Mentor, Companion, Engineer, Innovator.

    Priority

    Ensure personality is visible but never blocks clarity, precision, or regulatory wording. If there is a conflict, clarity wins.
